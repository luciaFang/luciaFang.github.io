OBJECTIVE

One of the biggest challenges in sign language translation is the lack of technology supporting translation between different regions. To address this issue, under the supervision of my mentor, we have developed a no-code web interface for a state-of-the-art hand tracking algorithm called Mediapipe, which can track hands in videos with high accuracy. To further enhance our approach, we have developed a new method to track time points with corresponding 3D hand poses. Building on our progress from last semester, I am currently collecting more American, Spanish, and Chinese sign language videos. Our goal is to build a supervised Convolutional Neural Network that can read finger-spelling for each language. To facilitate sign translation across languages, we will leverage the Google Translate API, followed by text to image reconstruction to provide a more intuitive sign language translation. Our project has the potential to make a significant impact in improving accessibility and communication for the deaf and hard of hearing community.

The purpose of the research was to develop a system capable of recognizing American Sign language
(ASL) finger-spelling in real time. People who are deaf or hard of hearing use finger-spelling to
communicate, in which each letter of the alphabet is represented by a unique hand gesture.
In our pipeline, video input of a person’s finger-spelling is analyzed using machine learning algorithms,
and the corresponding letters are outputted in real-time, enabling people who use finger-spelling to
communicate with others who are unfamiliar with the gestures. Our pipeline can facilitate
communication and improve the lives of people who are deaf or hard of hearing by decoding
finger-spelling in real time.

3D POSE ESTIMATION OF HAND FOR ASL

Our research utilized a video of a person finger spelling to extract 3D pose information through Mediapipe. Theextraction allowed us to determine which letters were being represented and how the hand pose changed over time.The plotted 3D coordinates of 24 out of the 26 letters as well as ”background” of the alphabet provide a visualrepresentation of the extracted data (Fig. 2a), which can be used to analyze and improve the finger-spelling de-coding algorithm’s accuracy. By using the plotted 3D coordinates as a ”reduced” set of features, we can develop afinger-spelling classifier for American Sign Language (ASL). This approach generalizes across different hand sizes,video resolutions, camera angles, and backgrounds. The classifier’s versatility enables accurate decoding of finger-spelling in various situations, making it a more useful tool

RESULTS
Development of an user-interface

Mediapipe is a machine learning framework that can decode hand skeletons in real-time which is an useful tool to decode the meaning of the sign language. In order to make it easier to use Mediapipe for this purpose, we developed a user interface that simplifies the process of decoding sign language from video input, making it easier to use. An interactive web-based application was designed to allow the user upload a sign video, and then watch the replay with predicted hand skeleton superimposed.

As a result, the user will be able to see exactly how the hand pose is being interpreted by the system and can adjust it as necessary to improve the accuracy of the decoded sign language that is being produced. Furthermore, the user interface includes tools for post-processing the decoded hand skeleton data, such as creating a 2D convolution neural network that maps these hand pose images to signed alphabet. Overall, our user-interface makes it easy for anyone to use Mediapipe to decode signlanguage in real-time.

Convolution Neural Network training and prediction

With 3D pose and it’s corresponding alphabet, we opted to train a 2D convolution neural network. First, we projected these 3D hand pose (Fig. 2a) onto 2D and down-sample the high resolution images to 128x128 pixels to be able to generalize hand shape and subtle angle differences between signers.

Across two videos, we split into 70%train and 30% test. Upon 20 training epochs, the validation accuracy plateaued and reached 90% (Fig. 2b). Next, we sought to investigate the mistakes from each alphabet and background (BG) and found that, as expected, stationary signs without occlusion of fingers works well; whereas moving signs such as ”J”, and ”Z”, along with occluded sign like ”O” were often times confused with background (Fig. 2c).

SETBACKS

Mediapipe is a machine learning framework designed to predict hand poses in videos using prebuilt supervised neural network classifiers. It allows for predicting the hand pose in every frame of a video, and the predicted pose can be overlaid on the input video frame as a reference. However, the only output provided to the end-user is real-time prediction of the hand pose, with limited support for post-hoc analysis of the data. As a result, in experienced users may find it challenging to annotate, quantify, and classify various signs in a video, particularly in continuous streams. Nonetheless, it is possible to work around these limitations by writing the hand pose and the corresponding frame number to a writable data file, enabling the user to revisit specific time points of a video for further analysis. While Mediapipe’s lack of support for post-hoc analysis is a limitation, it remains a useful tool for processing and visualizing an entire video, provided users are willing to use workarounds.3

CONCLUSION

We developed a user-friendly, front-end web application that incorporates Mediapipe’s real-time hand pose prediction with post-hoc quantification and visualization scripts to increase the usability of cutting-edge technology. Specifically, we focused on ASL finger-spelling decoding and demonstrated diverse 3D hand poses across all alpha-bets. With this application, users can upload a video of themselves signing in ASL, and the algorithm will deter-mine the hand position at each frame. The retrieved hand pose information can then be compared and contrasted with one another. Additionally, we leveraged the 3D pose generated from Mediapipe to enable a convolution neural network to learn the signed alphabet from the hand pose images. The network achieved a validation accuracy of 90%. However, we noted that the network fails when the alphabet sign is non-stationary, or the hand isn’t fully visible. Ultimately, by combining Mediapipe’s hand pose prediction with another layer of 2D convolution neural network, we created a powerful tool for improving ASL finger-spelling classification, benefiting the deaf and hardof hearing community.

PROSPECT FOR THE FUTURE

The next step is to develop a real-time sign language translation app that has the potential to revolutionize communication for the deaf community on a global scale. What we envision is an improved version of the app that utilize a video stream that captures an image every half a second, which will then be fed into Mediapipe to identify 3D hand pose. This information will be passed through our trained convolution neural network, which will output the corresponding English alphabet. By stacking these alphabets and segmenting them with 1-2 seconds of background classification, we will construct English words and formulate a sentence. Once the sentence is complete, the app will translate the text into the language of the user's choice. To complete the communication loop, we will add text-to-speech functionality. By integrating sign language decoding, text translation, and text-to-speech, our app has the potential to facilitate seamless communication between the deaf and non-deaf populations, regardless of language barriers.
