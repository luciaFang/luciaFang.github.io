FALL 2022 | UX DESIGN

One of the biggest challenges in sign language translation is the lack of technology supporting translation between different regions. To address this issue, under the supervision of my mentor, we have developed a no-code web interface for a state-of-the-art hand tracking algorithm called Mediapipe, which can track hands in videos with high accuracy. To further enhance our approach, we have developed a new method to track time points with corresponding 3D hand poses. Building on our progress from last semester, I am currently collecting more American, Spanish, and Chinese sign language videos. Our goal is to build a supervised Convolutional Neural Network that can read finger-spelling for each language. To facilitate sign translation across languages, we will leverage the Google Translate API, followed by text to image reconstruction to provide a more intuitive sign language translation. Our project has the potential to make a significant impact in improving accessibility and communication for the deaf and hard of hearing community.

The purpose of the research was to develop a system capable of recognizing American Sign language
(ASL) finger-spelling in real time. People who are deaf or hard of hearing use finger-spelling to
communicate, in which each letter of the alphabet is represented by a unique hand gesture.
In our pipeline, video input of a person’s finger-spelling is analyzed using machine learning algorithms,
and the corresponding letters are outputted in real-time, enabling people who use finger-spelling to
communicate with others who are unfamiliar with the gestures. Our pipeline can facilitate
communication and improve the lives of people who are deaf or hard of hearing by decoding
finger-spelling in real time.

OBJECTIVE

3D POSE ESTIMATION OF HAND FOR ASL

The video of a person finger spelling was used in our research to extract 3D pose information by using Mediapipe. Based on the analysis of the video and the extraction of the 3D pose of the person’s hands, we determined which letters were being represented, and observed how the hand pose changed over time. This example shows the 3D coordinates for 24 out of the 26 letters of the alphabet. This visual representation of the extracted 3D pose data can be useful for analyzing and improving the accuracy of the finger-spelling decoding algorithm (Fig. 1C). The plotted 3D coordinates can be used to develop a finger-spelling classifier for American Sign Language (ASL). We can use the plotted 3D coordinates as a ”reduced” set of features to generalize across different hand sizes, video resolutions, camera angles, and backgrounds. This will enable the classifier to accurately decode finger-spelling in a variety of different situations, making it more useful and versatile.

SETBACKS: LIMITED APPLICABILITY WITH DEFAULT OUTPUT

Mediapipe is a machine learning framework that is used for predicting hand poses in videos based on machine learning. Every frame of the video is capable of predicting the hand pose, and the predicted pose can be superimposed over the input video frame as a reference. Real-time prediction of hand pose is the only output that is provided to the end user, and there is little support for post-hoc analyses of the data that could be carried out. Therefore, Mediapipe’s output can be very difficult for inexperienced users to annotate, quantify, and classify various signs presented throughout a video, let alone continuous streams. In spite of these limitations, we managed to find workarounds in order to process and visualize an entire video using Mediapipe. Specifically, we write the hand pose and the corresponding frame number to a writable data file. This allows the user to revisit particular time points of a video for further data quantification and analyses. In summary, Mediapipe does not provide a lot of support for post-hoc analysis, but it is possible to workaround the lack of support and use the framework to process and visualize an entire video using the framework.

PROSPECT FOR THE FUTURE

Our next goal is to decode sign language using pose clustering. In order to achieve this, we will be using machine learning algorithms to group similar hand poses together and to identify the relationships between different hand poses. In order to predict the finger-spelled letter from a video of a person signing in ASL, we can train a classifier to analyze these pose-relationships and then use the classifier to estimate the finger-spelled letter from the video. It is also possible to use a pre-built language model for the purpose of correcting a decoding error in the event that an error occurs during the decoding process. In order to make suggestions for the correction of any mistakes or inaccuracies that are found in the decoded sign language text, the language model must be fed the decoded sign language text. With the combination of pose clustering and language modeling, we can potentially create a system that is highly accurate and robust in decoding sign language from a webcam.
